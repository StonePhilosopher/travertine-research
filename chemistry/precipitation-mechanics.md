# Travertine Chemistry and Physics

## Core Reaction

The fundamental chemistry:

```
Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ ⇌ CaCO₃↓ + H₂O + CO₂↑
```

**Left side** (underground): Water dissolves calcium carbonate from limestone bedrock, picking up Ca²⁺ and HCO₃⁻ ions.

**Right side** (at surface): As water reaches the surface and CO₂ degasses, the equilibrium shifts, precipitating solid calcium carbonate (travertine).

## CO₂ Degassing as the Driver

Precipitation is not driven by evaporation (though it helps). The primary driver is **CO₂ loss**:

1. Deep water contains dissolved CO₂ from magmatic sources and organic decomposition
2. At the surface, CO₂ escapes to the atmosphere
3. CO₂ loss shifts carbonate equilibrium toward precipitation
4. More CO₂ lost = more CaCO₃ precipitated

**Thin-sheet flow over rims promotes maximum CO₂ loss** — this is why rims build up faster than pool bottoms.

## Aragonite vs Calcite

Temperature controls polymorph:

| Condition | Dominant Polymorph |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Hotter (>~60°C) | Aragonite |
| Cooler (<~60°C) | Calcite |
| Very hot (>80°C) | Aragonite dominates |
| Rapid precipitation | Aragonite favored (kinetic) |
| Slow precipitation | Calcite favored (thermodynamic) |

**At Mammoth:** ~170°F surface water favors aragonite, but cooling along flow paths creates calcite in distal areas.

## Supersaturation

- Deep water is saturated or slightly undersaturated at high pCO₂
- Surface water becomes supersaturated as CO₂ degasses
- Supersaturation ratio (Ω) determines precipitation rate
- Ω = [Ca²⁺][CO₃²⁻] / Ksp
  - Ω > 1 = supersaturated, precipitation possible
  - Ω >> 1 = rapid, often aragonite (kinetic control)
  - Ω slightly > 1 = slow, calcite favored (thermodynamic control)

## Surface Tension Controls Terrace Height

The Hammer et al. (2007/2010) model identifies a critical geometric relationship:

**IDD = h tan(α) + k**

Where:
- **IDD** = inter-dam distance (spacing between terraces)
- **h ≈ 4-5 mm** = terrace height — constant, set by surface tension meniscus
- **α** = slope angle
- **k** = minimum basal distance

**Key insight:** Terrace height is fixed by physics (surface tension of water), not by flow rate or chemistry. Wider terraces form on gentler slopes; narrower terraces on steeper slopes.

## Flow Regime and Precipitation Localization

| Feature | Flow | Depth | Transport | Texture |
|---------|------|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Pools** | Slow | Deep | Diffusion-dominated | Porous, botryoidal, stromatolitic |
| **Rims** | Fast (thin sheet) | Shallow | Advection-dominated | Compact, dense |
| **Outer walls** | Cascading | Variable | Turbulent | Layered, laminated |

**Precipitation is enhanced at rims** relative to pools because:
1. Thin sheet = maximum surface area for CO₂ exchange
2. Turbulence promotes CO₂ degassing
3. Constant replenishment of supersaturated water

## Biological Influence

Thermophilic microbial mats create color banding:

| Temperature Zone | Dominant Organisms | Color |
|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| Hottest (>73°C) | Chemolithotrophs | White/cream |
| Hot (60-73°C) | Synechococcus | Yellow-orange |
| Warm (40-60°C) | Chloroflexus | Green-brown |
| Cool (<40°C) | Mixed algae/cyanobacteria | Dark green-black |

Pigments (carotenoids, chlorophyll) stain the fresh precipitate, creating the characteristic Mammoth color bands.

## Deposition Rates

- Natural Mammoth: millimeters to centimeters per year
- Optimal conditions: several cm/year
- Pool areas: slower (diffusion-limited)
- Rim areas: faster (advection + degassing)

## Erosion and Dissolution

Travertine is not purely accretionary:
- Acid rain dissolves surface CaCO₃
- Pool water can become undersaturated and dissolve terrace floors
- Biological activity (algae, bacteria) can etch surfaces
- Seismic events can fracture and collapse terraces
